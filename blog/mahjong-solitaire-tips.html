Mahjong Solitaire Tips: How to Clear the Turtle

Published July 12, 2026

Mahjong Solitaire looks like a memory game and plays like a logistics problem. The rules fit in a sentence — match pairs of identical free tiles until the 144-tile turtle is gone — but the difference between clearing 60 tiles and clearing all of them is almost entirely which pair you take when you have a choice. These are the habits that matter, in order of impact. You can practice them on our free Mahjong Solitaire, where every deal is generated to be solvable — if you dead-end, it was a decision, not the shuffle.

1. Know what "free" really means

A tile is playable when nothing rests on top of it and at least one of its left or right sides is open. Top and bottom edges never matter. Most beginner mistakes are misreads of this rule — especially missing that a tile in the long middle row is side-locked even though its top is clear. When in doubt, tap it: locked tiles won't select.

2. Attack the blockers, not the easy pairs

Every removal is a chance to open new tiles, and positions differ wildly in how much they unlock. The peak tile pins four tiles under it; the long 14-tile row and the left/right wings seal entire flanks. An easy pair on the bottom edge opens nothing. When two pairs are available, take the one that digs into the tallest stack — think of the score as "tiles freed per move", not "tiles removed".

3. Count to four before you pair

Every tile face exists exactly four times, which means each face forms two pairs — and pairing the wrong two of three visible copies can strand the fourth under a mountain. The rule: when three copies are free (or visible), check where the fourth is before you match. Pair the two whose removal doesn't orphan the buried one. And remember the special groups: any 🌸 flower matches any flower, any 🍂 season any season — they can never strand each other.

4. Two identical tiles stacked? Leave them for last

If the last two copies of a face sit directly on top of each other, they can always be taken later (remove the top one's cover, then the pair). But if one lies beside the other's cover, you can build a genuine deadlock. This is the classic self-inflicted loss — when you notice twins touching, resolve their neighborhood early.

5. Undo beats shuffle, every time

On our boards, a winning sequence exists from the very first move — the deal is produced by running a legal disassembly in reverse. That means undo preserves the guaranteed win and shuffle throws it away for a random reroll. When you stall, step back a few moves and spend your pairs on the other flank instead. Treat shuffle as the entertainment button, not the strategy button.

6. Keep both flanks moving

The turtle punishes lopsided play: if you strip one side to the felt while the other stays tall, the tall side's tiles have fewer partners left in the pool. Alternate flanks and keep the number of free tiles high — more open tiles means more pairing options per turn, which compounds just like in TriPeaks.

Frequently asked

  • Is Mahjong Solitaire the same as mahjong? No — it borrows the beautiful tiles from the four-player table game, but it's a single-player matching puzzle invented in its computer form in the 1980s (often called Shanghai or Taipei).
  • Are all deals winnable? On most sites, no — blind shuffles produce a share of dead boards. Ours are dealt by simulating a legal disassembly backwards, so a winning line always exists (the same philosophy as our no-guess logic puzzles).
  • Flowers and seasons? Two wildcard-ish groups of four: flowers match any flower, seasons match any season. Grab them early — they're pure tempo.
