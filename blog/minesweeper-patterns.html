Minesweeper Patterns: How to Win Without Guessing

Published July 12, 2026

Minesweeper has a reputation as a luck game, and it's undeserved — at least until the last few cells. Nearly every move on a Minesweeper board is decidable by a handful of recurring patterns, and players who know them clear intermediate boards on instinct. This guide walks through the patterns in the order you should learn them. If you want to practice on boards where these patterns are guaranteed to be enough — no 50/50 coin-flips, ever — play our no-guess Minesweeper: its generator verifies every board with a logic solver before you see it.

The two reflexes: satisfied and tight-fit numbers

Ninety percent of Minesweeper is two symmetrical observations:

  • Satisfied number → everything else is safe. If a "2" already touches two flags, every other covered neighbor is guaranteed clear. Open them all (in most versions, clicking the satisfied number itself "chords" them open at once).
  • Tight fit → everything is a mine. If a "3" has exactly three covered neighbors left, all three are mines. Flag them without thinking.

Play a few games using only these two rules and you'll be surprised how far they carry you. Speed comes from applying them the instant a number's neighborhood changes.

The 1-1 pattern

Two 1s side by side along an unopened wall, where the first 1's covered cells are a subset of the second's: the first 1's mine must be among the shared cells, which satisfies the second 1 too — so any extra cell only the second 1 sees is safe. This is the pattern that opens up long straight borders.

The 1-2 pattern

A 1 and a 2 adjacent along a wall, sharing two covered cells, with the 2 seeing one extra cell: the 1 allows at most one mine among the shared cells, but the 2 needs two in total — so the extra cell is always a mine. Its famous cousin 1-2-1 places mines under both 1s; 1-2-2-1 places them under the 2s. You don't need to memorize the variants if you remember the reasoning: compare what two overlapping numbers can account for.

Subset logic (the general form)

All the named patterns are special cases of one idea. Take two numbers A and B whose covered neighborhoods overlap, where A's cells are contained in B's. Then:

  • If A and B need the same number of mines, B's extra cells are safe.
  • If B needs exactly as many more mines as it has extra cells, the extra cells are all mines.

Once this clicks, you stop seeing "patterns" and start seeing arithmetic. Our Minesweeper's hint system names these deductions as it shows them — first press explains which numbers force the move and why, second press plays it — which is a fast way to train the eye.

Endgame: count the mines

The 🚩 counter is a constraint, not decoration. When the remaining mine count equals the number of covered cells, they're all mines; when it hits zero, everything left is safe. Many "unsolvable" endgames fall instantly to this global count — check it whenever local numbers stop moving.

What about real 50/50s?

On classic random boards, sometimes no pattern helps — two identical cells, one mine, pick one. That's not a skill failure; the board genuinely forces a guess. It's also fixable at the source: our Minesweeper plays every candidate board with a solver that uses exactly the deductions above (plus the mine counter), and if the solver would have to guess, mines are relocated until it doesn't. The result: if you lose, there was always a findable move — which turns every defeat into a lesson. That guarantee is the same no-guess standard we apply to all our logic games.

Quick habits that add up

  • Flag only what you've proven. A wrong flag silently corrupts every number that touches it.
  • Chord aggressively. Satisfied numbers open multiple cells at once — it's both faster and safer than clicking cells individually.
  • Work the border, not the middle. New information only appears where numbers meet covered cells.
  • Re-scan after every flag. Each new flag can satisfy several numbers at once — collect all the free opens before moving on.
